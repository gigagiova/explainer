In 1915, Sewall Wright sat in a US Department of Agriculture office in
Beltsville, Maryland, with a stack of guinea-pig pedigrees in front of
him. The herd had been inbred for ten generations to study the effects
of close mating on body weight, vigor, and coat color. Everyone could
see the animals were getting smaller and stranger. Nobody had a clean
way to *measure how inbred they were*.

The problem looks innocent. Pick any animal in the pedigree. Its mother
and father might be siblings, or half-siblings, or cousins — or, more
often, some intricate mixture, because the herd had been mating within
itself for years. How do you collapse that whole tangled history into a
single number?

Wright's answer, published in 1921 and 1922, was the **inbreeding
coefficient** $F$, together with a graphical method — **path analysis**
— invented expressly to compute it. The two ideas are inseparable: $F$
is *defined* by the paths you can trace in a pedigree.

## What we want $F$ to mean

Before deriving anything, fix the picture. An animal has two copies of
each gene, one from each parent. Call those two copies *identical by
descent* (IBD) when they are both physical replicas of a single copy
present in some shared ancestor.

<div className="callout">
**Definition.** The inbreeding coefficient $F$ of an individual is the
probability that its two gene copies, at a randomly chosen locus, are
identical by descent.
</div>

A wholly outbred animal has $F = 0$: the maternal and paternal copies
trace back to unrelated ancestors. An animal whose parents are full
siblings has $F = 1/4$. An animal that is the result of self-fertilization
of an outbred parent has $F = 1/2$. After many generations of brother–
sister mating, $F$ approaches $1$, and the animal is essentially
homozygous everywhere.

The number is doing real work. It tells you how much of the genome has
collapsed onto identical pairs of copies — and so, indirectly, how
much hidden recessive variation has been exposed.

## The single-ancestor case, by sheer counting

Start with the simplest non-trivial pedigree. Two parents $X$ and $Y$
share *exactly one* common ancestor $A$. Mate $X$ and $Y$ and ask: with
what probability are the two gene copies in their offspring $O$
identical by descent?

Pick any one gene copy in $A$. Trace its journey down to $O$.

1. With probability $1/2$, $A$ passes that copy (rather than the
   other) to its child on the path toward $X$.
2. The child passes it on, again with probability $1/2$, to *its* child.
3. ...and so on, until the gene reaches $X$, then $X$ passes it on to
   $O$ — also with probability $1/2$.

If the path from $A$ to $X$ has $n_1$ links (parent–child steps), the
probability that a particular gene copy in $A$ ends up in $O$ via this
side is $(1/2)^{n_1 + 1}$ — one factor of $1/2$ per link, plus one for
the final transmission from $X$ to $O$.

Symmetrically, the probability that the *same* copy in $A$ ends up in
$O$ via the other side is $(1/2)^{n_2 + 1}$.

The two sides are independent transmissions, so the probability that
both gene copies in $O$ trace back to *that one specific copy* in $A$ is

$$
\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{n_1 + 1} \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{n_2 + 1}
= \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{n_1 + n_2 + 2}.
$$

But $A$ has *two* copies of the gene, and either of them could be the
shared one. Doubling:

$$
\Pr(\text{IBD via this specific copy of } A) \times 2
= \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{n_1 + n_2 + 1}.
$$

That doubling is the entire reason a $1/2$ — not a $1/4$ — appears at
the front of Wright's formula. Two parents only need to share *one
copy* of $A$, and there are two ways that can happen.

## A correction: $A$ may itself be inbred

So far we have assumed the two copies in $A$ are different. But what if
$A$ is itself inbred? Then there is an extra probability $f_A$ that the
two copies inside $A$ are *already* identical by descent — in which
case the doubling above slightly overcounts (the two copies are really
one). Working through the bookkeeping gives Wright's correction:

$$
F_O \;=\; \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{n_1 + n_2 + 1} (1 + f_A).
$$

A useful gut-check: full siblings have $n_1 = n_2 = 1$, so

$$
F = \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{1 + 1 + 1} (1 + 0) = \tfrac{1}{8}
$$

— *per common ancestor*. Full sibs share both parents, so we add two
copies of this contribution and recover the famous $F = 1/4$.

## Many ancestors: just add the paths

The leap that turned this trick into a method is small but decisive.
If $X$ and $Y$ share *several* common ancestors $A_1, A_2, \dots$, each
one contributes its own term — provided you only count paths that pass
through a single common ancestor and that don't reuse any individual.
Wright's general formula reads

$$
F_O \;=\; \sum_{k} \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{n_{1k} + n_{2k} + 1}
                    (1 + f_{A_k}),
$$

where the sum runs over all valid paths and $n_{1k}, n_{2k}$ are the
two leg-lengths of the $k$-th path. The whole inbreeding question
reduces to *enumerating loops in a pedigree* and reading off two
numbers per loop.

## The leap: path coefficients in general

Wright noticed, between 1918 and 1921, that the same accounting works
far beyond inbreeding. Replace "transmission of a gene copy" with
"propagation of a standardized causal effect", and $1/2$ with an
arbitrary *path coefficient* on each arrow, and the formula
$\sum (\text{product of path coefficients along a path})$ becomes a
*general* recipe for decomposing a correlation into the contributions of
the causal channels that produce it.

That is **path analysis**. It is the ancestor of structural equation
modeling, of much of modern causal inference, and — through Wright's
own students — of a sizable portion of twentieth-century biometry.
The whole framework was bootstrapped by trying to count gene copies in
a guinea-pig herd.

## Why this argument is satisfying

Three features make Wright's derivation feel almost inevitable once you
see it:

- **The picture is the proof.** A path from $A$ down to $O$ is both a
  visual object and a sequence of independent $1/2$'s. The diagram
  *is* the multiplication.
- **The doubling is structural, not algebraic.** The factor of $1/2$
  out front, which looks mysterious in formula form, is just "either
  copy of $A$ might be the shared one."
- **The correction $(1 + f_A)$ encodes its own intuition.** If $A$ is
  itself inbred, its two copies are partly the same gene, so paths
  that we counted as distinct partially coincide; the inflation
  factor is exactly the probability that they do.

Once those three ideas are in your hands, the general path-analysis
formula stops looking like an identity to memorize and starts looking
like the only sensible bookkeeping.
