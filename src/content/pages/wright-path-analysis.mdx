In 1915, Sewall Wright sat in a US Department of Agriculture office
with a stack of guinea-pig pedigrees in front of him. The herd had been
inbred for ten generations to study the effects of close mating on body
weight, vigor, and coat color. Everyone could see the animals were
getting smaller and stranger. Nobody had a clean way to *measure how
inbred they were*.

The reason it was hard is in the picture below. An outbred animal's
ancestry is a clean binary tree: every great-grandparent is a distinct
individual, and the parents share no ancestors. After only two
generations of full-sib mating, the same diagram collapses onto a
handful of individuals, and any single animal inherits gene copies
along many redundant routes back to a tiny pool of founders.

<PedigreeContrast />

Wright's question was how to summarise that redundancy in one number.
He called the answer the inbreeding coefficient $F$. But the *method*
he invented to compute $F$ turned out to be much bigger than the
question that motivated it. By 1921 he had given the method a name,
**path analysis**, and turned it into a graphical accounting that
converts causal questions into arithmetic. Judea Pearl, in *The Book
of Why*, opens with this story because Wright's 1921 paper contains
the first written form of the calculus of causation. This piece is
about that calculus, with $F$ kept as the historical scaffold rather
than the destination.

## What we are after

Two variables are *related* when they are not independent: knowing
the value of one tells you something about the value of the other.
Without relations, every measurement would leave us exactly as
ignorant about everything else as we were before; observation could
carry no information, and action would have no foreseeable
consequence. Relations are the substance of every non-trivial
question we can ask about a system.

Two flavours of relation will run through this piece, and they are
not the same.

**Causal** relations are the ones we'd like to act on: tweak $X$ and
$Y$ moves with it. Picking a treatment, setting a price, breeding a
guinea-pig, any goal-directed action presupposes that the world
contains stable answers to *"if I do $X$, what will $Y$ do?"*
**Observational** relations are the ones we can actually see in
data. We watch a system, write down a lot of value pairs, and
notice that some configurations occur together more often than
others. The cleanest one-number summary of a linear observational
relation is the *correlation* $r_{XY}$, the tilt of the joint cloud
of $(X, Y)$ pairs once both variables are put on a common scale.

We care about correlations specifically because they are the form
of evidence that passive observation gives us. We cannot see a
"cause" directly; we can only see what tends to occur with what. So
if we want to reason about causes from data, and we usually do
because data is all we have, we have to know what correlations a
given causal structure would produce. *That* is what Wright's path
rule gives us. Different causal stories can leave identical
observational fingerprints (the bedrock under "correlation does not
imply causation"), but the forward direction is exact: given the
causes and their strengths, the rule predicts the correlations
precisely.

The rest of this piece derives that forward dictionary, starting
with a calculation about inbred guinea-pigs.

## A counting trick on a pedigree

Define the **inbreeding coefficient** $F_O$ of an animal $O$ as the
probability that $O$'s two gene copies at a randomly chosen locus are
physical descendants of one and the same gene copy in some ancestor
shared by $O$'s two parents $X$ and $Y$. When that happens we say $O$
is *identical by descent* (IBD) at that locus, and $F_O$ is the IBD
probability.

Take the simplest non-trivial setup: $X$ and $Y$ share exactly one
common ancestor $A$. Like every diploid individual, $A$ carries two
gene copies at this locus, one inherited from each of $A$'s parents.
Label them **copy 1** and **copy 2**. Assume the two copies are
physically distinct genes: $A$'s own parents shared no ancestor who
passed the same gene down to both of them. In genetics that
assumption has a name, *$A$ is outbred at this locus*. It is what
lets us treat copy 1 and copy 2 as two genuinely different things to
keep track of.

That is the contribution to $F_O$ from one shared ancestor. If $X$ and
$Y$ share several common ancestors, each contributes its own term by
the same argument, and the contributions add. The structural insight
is the useful part: *the answer factorises as a product of independent
steps along a path in the graph, summed over paths.*

That structure does not depend on the steps being gene transmissions,
or on the probability being $1/2$. It depends only on the path itself
and on each step contributing a multiplicative factor.

The bridge back to correlations is direct. $F$ itself is a correlation:
treat the two gene copies in $O$ as random variables and $F$ is
exactly the correlation between them. The $1/2$ per meiosis is the
path coefficient on the gene-transmission arrow. So the IBD
calculation we just stepped through is the path rule applied to one
specific tiny graph, with correlations standing in for probabilities.
What "What we are after" promised, the IBD case has already delivered;
the next section just generalises it. Wright noticed this between
1918 and 1921 and made the leap.

## The general rule

The trick that worked for inbreeding works much more widely. The "$1/2$
per meiosis" was specific to gene transmission, but the picture
underneath, *trace a path from one thing to another, multiply a small
number at each step, and add up the products when several paths
exist*, was not. The same picture governs how strongly any two
quantities in a chain of cause and effect are related, whatever the
quantities are. Step through the derivation, starting from a tiny
two-step chain.

Time to put names on what the stepper just showed. The graph itself is
called a **directed acyclic graph**, or *DAG*: a network of arrows
running from causes to effects, with no cycles allowed. We say each
variable is **standardised** when we put it on a common scale, mean $0$
and variance $1$, so that the numbers on the arrows are directly
comparable. With those conventions, every variable in the graph is a
linear function of its parents, plus an independent piece of noise:

$$
V \;=\; \sum_{U \to V} c_{UV}\, U \,+\, \varepsilon_V,
$$

The numbers $c_{UV}$ on the arrows are the **path coefficients**, and
the equation above is called a **structural equation**: it is a
*causal* claim, saying the parents of $V$ are exactly what determine
$V$'s value, with $c_{UV}$ measuring how much a one-unit change in
$U$ moves $V$ when the other parents are held fixed. The leftover
$\varepsilon_V$ is whatever else moves $V$, and we assume it is
independent of every variable upstream of $V$ in the graph.

The number we actually observe in data is the **correlation** $r_{XY}$,
the tilt of the joint cloud the stepper showed. Wright's rule is the
dictionary that translates between causes (the path coefficients on
the arrows) and observations (the correlations between variables):
feed in causes, get out correlations.

<div className="callout">
**Wright's path rule.** For any two variables $X$ and $Y$ in the graph,

$$
r_{XY} \;=\; \sum_{p \,:\, \text{open}} \;\prod_{e \in p} c_e.
$$

A path is *open* if it does not pass through a *collider*, a node where
two arrowheads meet ($\to V \leftarrow$). Otherwise the path is
*blocked*.
</div>

### Does the dictionary work both ways?

Forward, yes: given a DAG and its path coefficients, the rule predicts
$r_{XY}$ exactly. Backward, recovering causes from correlations alone,
is harder and *not always possible*. Different DAGs can imply the same
set of correlations. The chain $X \to M \to Y$, the reverse chain
$Y \to M \to X$, and the fork $X \leftarrow M \to Y$ all produce the
same correlation pattern between the three variables: each pair is
correlated, with the same products of path coefficients. Observational
data alone cannot tell them apart.

To break the tie you need either an *intervention* (a randomised
experiment that severs one of the arrows by setting a variable from
the outside) or *auxiliary structural assumptions* (temporal order,
known mechanism, an instrument, prior knowledge that some arrow is
absent). This asymmetry is why "correlation does not imply causation"
is true as a slogan: the same statistical signature can be produced by
multiple distinct causal stories, and you need information from
*outside* the correlation table to choose. Wright's rule is exact in
the forward direction, and Pearl's work, sixty years later, is largely
about pinning down which causal questions the inverse direction can
and cannot answer.

## Four diagrams

Four configurations exhaust the local structure of any DAG.

**Chain $X \to M \to Y$.** One open path, contribution $a \cdot b$.
Causation flows from $X$ through $M$ to $Y$ and the correlation is the
product of the two effects.

**Fork $X \leftarrow C \to Y$.** $X$ and $Y$ have no causal relation;
$C$ is a common cause. The path $X \leftarrow C \to Y$ is open (no
collider), contribution $a \cdot b$. This is *spurious correlation*:
ice-cream sales and drowning rates correlate through summer
temperature, not through any direct effect of one on the other.

**Direct plus mediated $X \to Y$ and $X \to M \to Y$.** Two open
paths. Contributions $c$ (direct) and $a \cdot b$ (indirect). Total
$r_{XY} = c + ab$. Wright introduced this decomposition to estimate
the relative magnitude of the direct effect and the indirect channel,
which is what mediation analysis still does.

**Collider $X \to Z \leftarrow Y$.** $X$ and $Y$ are independent. The
only path between them, $X \to Z \leftarrow Y$, has two arrowheads
meeting at $Z$. By the rule it is *blocked*. So $r_{XY} = 0$. Knowing
that $X$ and $Y$ both cause $Z$ gives you no correlation between $X$
and $Y$ themselves, which is the right answer.

## The arithmetic of conditioning

Wright's path rule covers the *unconditional* correlation between two
variables. What happens when we restrict our attention to a subset of
the data with a specific value of a third variable, or include that
variable as a covariate in a regression? This is *conditioning*, and
it does very different things depending on where the conditioned
node sits in the DAG. In some configurations it removes a
correlation, in others it creates one out of nothing.

Three local configurations cover every case. Walk through them one
at a time. For each, drag the path coefficients to set up the graph,
and tick *Condition on …* to watch what happens to the $(X, Y)$
joint cloud on the right.

### Chain: $X \to M \to Y$, condition on $M$

A chain. $X$ causes $M$, $M$ causes $Y$, and that is the only route
between $X$ and $Y$. $M$, the variable through which the effect from
$X$ flows on its way to $Y$, is called the *mediator*. Without
conditioning, $X$ and $Y$ are correlated through the chain:
$r(X, Y) = a \cdot b$. Now suppose we restrict attention to data
where $M$ takes a specific value: any information that flowed from
$X$ to $Y$ went through $M$, and with $M$ held fixed that channel is
closed.

<ChainSliceScatter client:load />

The result is $r(X, Y \mid M) = 0$, whatever $a$ and $b$ are. In
statistics this is called *controlling for the mediator*; in a
regression, including $M$ as a covariate produces exactly the same
collapse.

### Fork: $X \leftarrow C \to Y$, condition on $C$

A fork. $X$ and $Y$ have no direct effect on each other; they share
a *common cause* $C$, also called the *confounder* in regression
contexts. Without conditioning, $X$ and $Y$ are correlated, but the
correlation is *spurious*: neither variable causes the other, they
just both depend on $C$. Conditioning on $C$, fixing it to a
specific value, removes the source of shared variation.

<ForkSliceScatter client:load />

The result is $r(X, Y \mid C) = 0$, whatever $a$ and $b$ are. In
statistics this is called *controlling for the confounder*; including
$C$ as a regression covariate produces the same collapse. It is the
single most common adjustment in applied statistics.

### Collider: $X \to Z \leftarrow Y$, condition on $Z$

A collider. $X$ and $Y$ are independent and both cause $Z$. Without
conditioning, no information flows between $X$ and $Y$: the only
path between them, $X \to Z \leftarrow Y$, is blocked by the
head-to-head meeting at $Z$. So $r(X, Y) = 0$.

But this is the surprising case. *Conditioning on $Z$ opens the
path*, and a correlation appears between $X$ and $Y$ out of nothing.
This pattern shows up under another name in applied work,
*selection bias* (or *Berkson's paradox* in its medical form), and
it happens whenever we restrict our attention to a subset of data
defined by a downstream variable that two upstream traits jointly
influence.

#### Admissions: smart and charm

The classic example is college admissions. Imagine a college that
admits applicants on the strength of two independent traits,
intelligence and charm. Bright-and-charming applicants get in
easily; dull-and-dull applicants are rejected; bright-but-dull and
charming-but-dull applicants compete for the marginal seats. Among
the *admitted* students, intelligence and charm are *negatively*
correlated, even though they are independent in the applicant pool.

The applicant pool's correlation hovers around zero. The admitted

*level set* of the admission rule: every $(s, c)$ on it satisfies
$a\,s + b\,c = t$, and its slope is $-a/b$. A point further right
along the line has a lower charm to keep the weighted sum constant,
which is the geometric origin of the negative correlation in the
admitted cloud. Drag $a$ and $b$ to tilt the line, and watch the
cloud's correlation respond: the closer $a$ and $b$ are to each
other, the more bend the admitted subset picks up.

This is the collider case playing out on real-looking data:
admission is the collider, smart and charm both flow into it, and
conditioning on admission opens the path between them. The same
pattern recurs everywhere we stratify by an outcome that two
upstream traits jointly produce:

- **Hospital populations.** Two diseases independent in the general
  public can appear negatively correlated among hospital patients,
  because hospital admission is more likely if you have at least one
  of them.
- **Promoted employees.** Within a company's senior ranks, technical
  skill and political savvy might appear anti-correlated, even if
  the underlying workforce shows no such pattern, because both
  routes get you promoted.
- **Surviving treatments.** Among patients alive five years after
  diagnosis, traits that helped them survive can look anti-correlated
  with traits that hurt them, because making it through required
  enough of *some* combination.

The structural pattern is identical to the admissions case: two
independent traits, a downstream variable they both influence, and
a sample restricted by that variable. The figure made the negative
correlation visible; the algebra below shows where its exact value

The minus sign is the whole story. If $Z = aX + bY$ is held fixed
at some observed value, a high $X$ forces a low $Y$ to compensate,
and vice versa: the constraint links the two causes negatively.

### What this means in practice

Conditioning is what most everyday statistical work does. Including a
variable as a covariate in a regression is conditioning on it.
Stratifying a study population by some characteristic is conditioning
on it. Filtering data by an outcome (admitted, hospitalised, alive at
follow-up, employed) is conditioning on it.

That makes the collider rule operationally important. The first
instinct is often to "control for everything we can measure", but
controlling for a collider *introduces* spurious correlations rather
than removing them. The careful version of "control for confounders"
is "control for the right confounders", which means thinking about
the causal graph before choosing the regression. Pearl's *back-door
criterion* makes this precise: the variables you should condition on
to estimate a causal effect of $X$ on $Y$ are exactly those that
block all *non-causal* paths between them without opening any
collider paths.

Wright's path rule plus these conditioning rules are the
linear-Gaussian core of modern causal inference. Pearl's
*d-separation* generalises "open path" and "blocked path" to any
DAG, not just linear-Gaussian ones. The *do-calculus* extends
conditioning to *interventions*, where instead of restricting the
data to a subset we imagine actively setting a variable. Both
machineries sit on the foundation we have just derived: a correlation
between two variables in a DAG decomposes into a sum of contributions
from specific paths, and which paths count depends on the structure
of the graph and on what we observe.
